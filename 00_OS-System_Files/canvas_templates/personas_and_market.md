## The End User
> Uses the product daily. Cares about UX, speed, and not being blocked.
* **Role / Title:** [e.g., Mid-level DevOps Engineer / Platform Engineer]
* **Experience Level:** [e.g., 3-7 years. Knows the stack. Distrusts marketing. Judges by GitHub stars.]
* **Primary Pain:** [e.g., Spends 3+ hours per sprint debugging broken CI pipelines caused by YAML config drift]
* **Desired Gain:** [e.g., Ship features on Friday without dreading a broken pipeline ruining the weekend]

## The Champion
> Advocates internally for the product. Risks their political capital to push it through.
* **Role / Title:** [e.g., Head of Platform Engineering / Engineering Manager]
* **Career Motivation:** [e.g., Wants to be seen as the person who eliminated the #1 engineering bottleneck]
* **Primary Pain:** [e.g., Spends 20% of 1:1s dealing with CI pipeline complaints — it's destroying team morale]
* **Desired Gain:** [e.g., A solution that makes their team faster without adding another system to own]
* **Top Objection:** [e.g., "My VP will ask about security — is this SOC2 compliant?"]
* **Internal Sell:** [e.g., Must convince the Economic Buyer that the ROI exceeds the switching cost and security risk]

---

## The Economic Buyer
> Signs the check. Cares about risk, ROI, and strategic fit — rarely touches the product.
* **Role / Title:** [e.g., CTO / VP Engineering]
* **Primary Concern:** [e.g., Engineering velocity — losing 1 deployment cycle per week = $200k+ in delayed feature revenue]
* **Primary Pain:** [e.g., Cannot give leadership a clear answer on why the engineering team ships slower than competitors]
* **Desired Gain:** [e.g., Quantifiable improvement in deployment frequency and incident reduction]
* **Top Objection:** [e.g., "We're already paying for GitHub Actions — why is this different?"]
* **Deal-Breaker:** [e.g., Any vendor without SOC2 Type II is automatically disqualified by InfoSec]
